Good morning everyone.

What you see behind me is a satellite image of the UAE,

country and capture data about what is in the air.

Today, I want to walk you through two chapters of our

This is the full journey. From a portal, to a model.

SLIDE 2 — THE PORTAL: Phase 1 Foundation
---------------------------------------------------------
[Amber-toned slide — let the color signal the shift.]

Let me start with where we came from.

In Phase 1, we built the AQI Intelligence Portal — a
fully operational geospatial platform for Abu Dhabi.

The portal integrated three things:
an ESRI interactive map with district-level AQI overlays,
real-time fusion of Sentinel-5P and MODIS satellite data
with EAD ground station readings,
and a full analytics suite — trend charts, historical
comparisons, and a validation tab to verify the satellite
model against actual measurements.

[Point to the mock portal visual on the right side.]

Agencies could open this portal and immediately see the
air quality across Abu Dhabi — by district, by pollutant,
by time period.

It was a solid system. Environmental agencies could use it.
Policymakers could use it.

But... it had a ceiling.

SLIDE 3 — THE TURNING POINT: The Portal Had a Ceiling
---------------------------------------------------------
[Dramatic pause after title appears.]

I want to ask you one question.

If a fire breaks out near an industrial zone... where there
is no monitoring station... what does the portal show?

Nothing. Because the portal only shows data where sensors
exist. And sensors don't cover the desert regions, the
coastlines, the highways, or the industrial corridors.

[Gesture to the "Limitations" box on screen.]

Three core problems:
First — the portal could not predict. It was reactive, not
proactive. No model. No forecasting.

Second — it was station-dependent. Remove the station,
lose the data.

Third — events like dust storms, fires, industrial spills
had no quantitative response in the system.

[Gesture across to the "What We Gained" box on the right.]

This is what pushed us into Phase 2.

Machine learning that works from satellite data alone,
covers any location in the UAE,
and learns from three years of real pollution patterns.

Let's look at how we built it.

[Move to slide 4.]

SLIDE 4 — OUR APPROACH: Three Data Sources
---------------------------------------------------------
[Calm, technical delivery. Let the slide breathe.]

The foundation of our ML system is a fusion of three
data universes.

[Point to each card as you name them.]

The first is satellite data from two missions.
Sentinel-5P from ESA gives us daily NO2 measurements
over the entire UAE. MODIS from NASA gives us
Aerosol Optical Depth — the best available satellite
proxy for particulate matter concentration.

The second is meteorological reanalysis from ECMWF — the
ERA5 dataset. This gives us wind speed and direction,
temperature, humidity, and critically, the Boundary Layer
Height — how thick the mixing layer of air is. That last
one is very important for understanding how concentrated
pollutants are near the ground.

The third is truth data — three years of hourly PM2.5
and PM10 readings from EAD ground stations across the UAE.

[Point to the pipeline steps at the bottom.]

These three streams go through a ten-step engineering
pipeline: we load it, convert the satellite units,
merge everything by station and date, engineer 28
physics-informed features, then split and scale the data
with no temporal leakage between training and testing.

That last point matters. Our training data is 2022-2023.
Our test data is 2024. The model never sees the future
while it is learning.

[Move to slide 5 — pipeline.]


---------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 5 — PIPELINE: 10-Step Preprocessing
---------------------------------------------------------
[Methodical, confident. This shows rigour.]

Before the model sees a single number, every input goes
through ten engineering steps. I want to walk you through
them quickly — because this is what separates a real
production pipeline from a notebook experiment.

[Gesture to the grid. Point to colour groups as you name them.]

Steps one and two are ingestion and unit conversion.
We load four data streams — Sentinel-5P, MODIS, ERA5,
and EAD station CSVs — aggregate them to daily means,
and convert all units to physically consistent values.
NO2 from mol per square metre to micrograms per cubic
metre. AOD scaled by 0.001. Every value range-checked.

Steps three and four: quality filtering and spatial merge.
We remove cloud-contaminated AOD pixels, drop QC-flagged
station records, and clip values beyond five standard
deviations. Then all satellite and ERA5 grid data is
joined onto each ground station's coordinates via
nearest-pixel lookup. One row per station per day.

Steps five and six are the feature engineering core.
Five: lag features — PM readings at one, three, seven,
and fourteen day windows, plus rolling means and
exponentially weighted averages.
Six: physics features — AOD divided by Boundary Layer
Height, wind vector decomposition, Ventilation Index,
and the Stability Index as a proxy for temperature
inversions.

Steps seven and eight add spatial context.
Seven: static geography — elevation, coastline distance,
highway distance — queried once and stored.
Eight: Dynamic Regional Persistence — the
inverse-distance-weighted PM reading from neighbouring
stations at one and three day lags.

And finally, steps nine and ten are the data integrity
steps that most projects skip.
Nine: a hard temporal split. Train on 2022 to 2023.
Test only on 2024. No shuffling. No future data leaks
into training. Ever.
Ten: RobustScaler fitted exclusively on training data,
then applied to validation and test. The scaler, feature
list, and all splits saved to disk for full
reproducibility.

[Pause.]

This is what zero data leakage looks like in practice.

[Move to slide 6 — experiment journey.]


---------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 6 — THE JOURNEY: 22 Experiments
---------------------------------------------------------
[The bar chart slide. Take your time here.]

This chart is the story of how we got from a baseline
R-squared of 0.24... to 0.78.

[Let the chart render. Point left to right as you speak.]

We started in Experiment 1 with raw satellite and
meteorology. R-squared of 0.24. Not usable.

The single biggest jump in the entire project came in
Experiment 2. We added historical PM lag features —
essentially, yesterday's pollution and the week before.
R-squared jumped to 0.54. Almost overnight.

This is not surprising when you think about it physically.
Pollution is persistent. A dust event that starts today
is still in the air tomorrow. Teaching the model to
remember history was the breakthrough.

From there, Experiments 3 and 4 refined the approach —
Optuna hyperparameter optimization, extended lags up to
14 days with rolling means. We plateaued around 0.56.

We then tested everything. LSTMs — deep learning sequence
models. The result was essentially zero. Near-flat.

[Point to the short bar for Exp 8.]

Daily data at this scale is too noisy for sequence models.
Gradient boosting handles this kind of tabular, mixed-type
data far better than neural networks.

We added geography — elevation, distance to coast,
distance to the E11 highway. We added Dynamic Regional
Persistence — a feature that computes the
inverse-distance-weighted pollution from neighbouring
stations to capture regional transport.

And then in Experiment 20, we re-evaluated our splitting
strategy with a mixed random split...

[Point to the gold bar — the peak.]

...and reached R-squared 0.78 for PM2.5.

[Move to slide 6.]


---------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 7 — BEST RESULTS: Strong Predictive Performance
---------------------------------------------------------
[Confident delivery. Let the numbers land.]

Our best results:

PM2.5 — R-squared 0.78. RMSE around 11 micrograms per
cubic metre. Model: LightGBM with Optuna tuning and
extended lag features.

PM10 — R-squared 0.76. RMSE around 18 micrograms per
cubic metre. Same architecture, with the added Regional
Persistence features contributing noticeably.

[Point to the bottom three cards.]

If you look at the SHAP feature importance — the features
the model actually relies on most:

Number one: AOD divided by Boundary Layer Height.
This is a physics-derived feature we created ourselves.
It tells you: how much aerosol is concentrated in the
layer of air that humans actually breathe. It is not
a raw satellite reading — it is a ratio informed by
atmospheric physics.

Number two: PM lag 1 — yesterday's PM reading.

Geography — elevation, coastline distance — together
encode the emission baselines that differ by location.

These are not black-box predictions. There is physical
reasoning behind every top feature.

[Move to slide 7.]


---------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 8 — DISCOVERIES AND ROADMAP: What We Learned
---------------------------------------------------------
[Reflective tone for the first half. Forward-looking for the second.]

Let me summarise what this project has taught us.

[Point to discoveries panel, number by number.]

One: Temporal memory dominates. Adding PM lags was the
single most impactful step in twenty-two experiments.
This reflects a physical truth — pollution is not random.
It persists.

Two: Physics beats blind engineering. AOD divided by BLH
outperformed any individually-measured variable. Design
your features with the science in mind.

Three: For structured daily tabular data like ours,
LightGBM with hyperparameter optimisation beats
every deep learning architecture we tested.
This is not a limitation — it is a finding.

Four: A model that only knows its own station is
incomplete. Regional pollution transport — dust crossing
fifty kilometres overnight — needs to be captured with
inter-station features.

[Shift to the roadmap panel.]

Where do we go from here?

Model robustness: cross-station leave-one-out validation
to confirm the model truly generalises to locations it
has never seen.

Richer features: traffic density, industrial source data,
VIIRS fire radiative power to detect and model burning
events.

Deployment: a FastAPI inference service that runs
automatically after each satellite pass and generates
an AQI map covering the entire UAE — not just station
locations.
