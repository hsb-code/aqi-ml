# AQI Methodology Notes

## What is AQI?

**Air Quality Index (AQI)**: A tool used by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and other agencies worldwide to communicate how polluted the air currently is or how polluted it is forecast to become. It translates air pollutant concentrations into a simple, color-coded scale (0-500) that corresponds to different levels of health concern.

**Why it matters**:
- Provides easy-to-understand air quality information to the public
- Helps people make informed decisions about outdoor activities
- Protects public health by providing warnings about harmful pollution levels
- Uses color-coding: Green (Good) → Yellow (Moderate) → Orange (Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups) → Red (Unhealthy) → Purple (Very Unhealthy) → Maroon (Hazardous)
- A value of 100 generally corresponds to the national air quality standard for a pollutant
- Higher values indicate greater health risks

## The 6 Key Pollutants

### 1. PM2.5 (Particulate Matter < 2.5 μm)

**What produces it?**
- **Combustion sources**: Burning of gasoline, oil, diesel fuel, coal, and wood
- **Fires**: Wildfires, agricultural burning, brush burning, waste burning
- **Vehicles**: Especially diesel engines and heavy-duty vehicles
- **Power plants**: Fossil fuel combustion for electricity
- **Industrial processes**: Manufacturing, construction activities
- **Domestic sources**: Wood-burning stoves, fireplaces, cooking, tobacco smoke

**Why is it dangerous?**
- Particles are so small (1/30th width of human hair) they can penetrate deep into lungs and enter bloodstream
- **Respiratory effects**: Asthma attacks, bronchitis, reduced lung function, respiratory infections
- **Cardiovascular effects**: Heart attacks, strokes, cardiovascular disease
- **Other impacts**: Premature death, lung cancer, COPD, diabetes, adverse birth outcomes
- **No safe level**: Even low concentrations pose health risks
- **Most deadly pollutant**: Contributed to over 4 million deaths globally in 2019

**Typical levels**:
- **Good (WHO guideline)**: Annual average ≤ 5 µg/m³, Daily ≤ 15 µg/m³
- **Good (EPA)**: 0-12.0 µg/m³ (AQI 0-50)
- **Moderate**: 12.1-35.4 µg/m³ (AQI 51-100)
- **Unhealthy for Sensitive**: 35.5-55.4 µg/m³ (AQI 101-150)
- **Unhealthy**: 55.5-150.4 µg/m³ (AQI 151-200)
- **Very Unhealthy**: 150.5-250.4 µg/m³ (AQI 201-300)
- **Hazardous**: > 250.5 µg/m³ (AQI 301-500)

---

### 2. PM10 (Particulate Matter < 10 μm)

**What produces it?**
- **Dust sources**: Construction sites, unpaved roads, agriculture
- **Mechanical processes**: Crushing, grinding operations
- **Natural sources**: Windblown dust, pollen, soil particles
- **Combustion**: Similar to PM2.5 but larger particles
- **Industrial activities**: Mining, cement production

**Why is it dangerous?**
- Particles lodge in airways and upper respiratory tract
- **Respiratory irritation**: Coughing, wheezing, difficulty breathing
- **Asthma aggravation**: Triggers attacks in asthmatics
- **Reduced lung function**: Especially in children and elderly
- **Less penetration than PM2.5**: Doesn't reach deep lung tissue or bloodstream as easily
- Children and people with respiratory diseases most affected

**Typical levels**:
- **Good**: 0-54 µg/m³ (AQI 0-50)
- **Moderate**: 55-154 µg/m³ (AQI 51-100)
- **Unhealthy for Sensitive**: 155-254 µg/m³ (AQI 101-150)
- **Unhealthy**: 255-354 µg/m³ (AQI 151-200)
- **Very Unhealthy**: 355-424 µg/m³ (AQI 201-300)

---

### 3. NO₂ (Nitrogen Dioxide)

**What produces it?**
- **Primary source**: Combustion of fossil fuels at high temperatures
- **Motor vehicles**: Cars, trucks, buses - especially diesel engines (biggest contributor in urban areas)
- **Power plants**: Coal, oil, and natural gas burning
- **Industrial facilities**: Chemical plants, manufacturing, metal refining
- **Indoor sources**: Gas stoves, furnaces, unvented heaters
- **Natural sources** (minor): Volcanoes, bacteria
- **Peak times**: Morning and afternoon rush hours

**Why is it dangerous?**
- **Respiratory damage**: Irritates and inflames airways
- **Asthma**: Aggravates existing asthma, may contribute to development of asthma
- **Respiratory infections**: Increased susceptibility, especially in children
- **Lung function**: Reduced lung capacity and function
- **Hospital admissions**: Increased for respiratory and cardiovascular diseases
- **Other effects**: Cardiovascular disease, diabetes, hypertension, stroke, COPD
- **Vulnerable groups**: Children, asthmatics, elderly, pregnant individuals, people with heart/lung disease

**Typical levels**:
- **EPA standard**: 1-hour ≤ 100 ppb, Annual ≤ 53 ppb
- **WHO guideline**: 24-hour ≤ 25 µg/m³, Annual ≤ 10 µg/m³
- **Urban roadside (UK 2024)**: 20.7 µg/m³ annual average
- **Urban background**: 13.1 µg/m³
- **Rural background**: 4.7 µg/m³
- **Near highways**: 2-3× higher than residential areas further away
- **Moderate pollution trigger**: > 200 µg/m³ (hourly)

---

### 4. SO₂ (Sulfur Dioxide)

**What produces it?**
- **Power plants**: Coal and oil-burning facilities (largest source)
- **Industrial processes**: Petroleum refining, metal smelting (aluminum, steel), fertilizer manufacturing
- **Shipping**: Marine vessels burning high-sulfur fuel
- **Natural sources**: Volcanic eruptions, geothermal activity
- **Diesel engines**: Older vehicles and equipment

**Why is it dangerous?**
- **Respiratory system**: Primary target organ
- **Immediate effects**: Throat and nose irritation, coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath
- **Bronchitis**: Inflammation of airways, excess mucus production
- **Asthma attacks**: Severe reactions, especially during physical activity
- **Lung function**: Decreased capacity and function
- **Cardiovascular disease**: Linked to heart problems
- **Vulnerable groups**: Asthmatics, people with chronic bronchitis/emphysema, children, older adults
- **High concentrations**: Can be life-threatening

**Typical levels**:
- **Good**: 0-35 ppb (AQI 0-50)
- **Moderate**: 36-75 ppb (AQI 51-100)
- **Unhealthy for Sensitive**: 76-185 ppb (AQI 101-150)
- **WHO guideline**: 24-hour ≤ 40 µg/m³, 10-minute ≤ 500 µg/m³

---

### 5. CO (Carbon Monoxide)

**What produces it?**
- **Incomplete combustion**: Any carbon-containing fuel that doesn't burn completely
- **Motor vehicles**: Gasoline and diesel engines (major urban source)
- **Power plants**: Fossil fuel combustion
- **Wildfires**: Forest fires, agricultural burning
- **Indoor sources**: Gas appliances (furnaces, ranges, water heaters), fireplaces, wood stoves, charcoal grills, generators, space heaters
- **Tobacco smoke**: Significant indoor contributor
- **Industrial**: Incinerators, manufacturing processes

**Why is it dangerous?**
- **Oxygen deprivation**: Binds to hemoglobin 200× better than oxygen, preventing oxygen delivery to organs
- **Mild exposure**: Fatigue, headaches, dizziness, confusion, impaired vision
- **Moderate exposure**: Nausea, vomiting, chest pain, coordination problems
- **Severe exposure**: Loss of consciousness, brain damage, heart attack, death
- **Chronic effects**: Cardiovascular disease, neurological damage
- **"Silent killer"**: Colorless, odorless - victims don't know they're exposed
- **Vulnerable groups**: Unborn babies, infants, elderly, people with cardiovascular disease, anemia, respiratory illness

**Typical levels**:
- **Good**: 0-4.4 ppm (AQI 0-50)
- **Moderate**: 4.5-9.4 ppm (AQI 51-100)
- **Unhealthy for Sensitive**: 9.5-12.4 ppm (AQI 101-150)
- **Unhealthy**: 12.5-15.4 ppm (AQI 151-200)
- **EPA 8-hour standard**: ≤ 9 ppm
- **EPA 1-hour standard**: ≤ 35 ppm

---

### 6. O₃ (Ozone)

**What produces it?**
- **Not directly emitted**: Secondary pollutant formed by chemical reactions
- **Precursors**: Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs), nitrogen oxides (NOx), methane
- **Sunlight required**: Reactions occur when sunlight hits precursor pollutants
- **VOC sources**: Motor vehicles, industrial plants, gasoline vapors, chemical solvents, paints
- **NOx sources**: Vehicles, power plants, industrial facilities
- **Peak times**: Afternoon/evening (after sunlight exposure), summer months
- **Ozone layer vs ground ozone**: Upper atmosphere ozone is protective; ground-level ozone is harmful

**Why is it dangerous?**
- **Respiratory irritation**: Airways, lungs, eyes, nose, throat inflammation
- **Asthma**: Triggers attacks, worsens symptoms
- **Lung function**: Reduced capacity, difficulty breathing deeply
- **Chronic effects**: Exacerbates chronic respiratory diseases (bronchitis, COPD), increases infection susceptibility
- **Cardiovascular**: Heart problems, increased hospitalizations, premature death
- **Active individuals at risk**: Exercising outdoors increases exposure
- **"Sunburn of lungs"**: Damages lung tissue similar to skin sunburn
- **Vulnerable groups**: Children (developing lungs), outdoor workers, people with asthma/COPD, elderly

**Typical levels**:
- **Good**: 0-54 ppb (AQI 0-50)
- **Moderate**: 55-70 ppb (AQI 51-100)
- **Unhealthy for Sensitive**: 71-85 ppb (AQI 101-150)
- **Unhealthy**: 86-105 ppb (AQI 151-200)
- **EPA 8-hour standard**: ≤ 70 ppb

## AQI Calculation Formula

```
AQI = [(I_high - I_low) / (BP_high - BP_low)] × (C - BP_low) + I_low
```

**What this means**:
This formula converts measured pollutant concentrations into a standardized AQI value between 0-500. It uses linear interpolation within specific concentration ranges (breakpoints).

**Variables**:
- **C**: Measured concentration of the pollutant (truncated/rounded as per EPA rules)
- **BP_low**: Breakpoint concentration ≤ C
- **BP_high**: Breakpoint concentration ≥ C
- **I_low**: AQI value corresponding to BP_low
- **I_high**: AQI value corresponding to BP_high

**How it works**:
1. Find which breakpoint range your measured concentration falls into
2. Apply linear interpolation to calculate exact AQI value within that range
3. Calculate AQI for each pollutant measured
4. **Report the highest AQI** among all pollutants as the overall AQI

---

## Example Calculation

**Given**: PM2.5 = 35.5 µg/m³ (24-hour average)

**Step 1**: Find the breakpoint range
- 35.5 falls in range: 35.5-55.4 µg/m³ (Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups)
- BP_low = 35.5, BP_high = 55.4
- I_low = 101, I_high = 150

**Step 2**: Apply formula
```
AQI = [(150 - 101) / (55.4 - 35.5)] × (35.5 - 35.5) + 101
AQI = [49 / 19.9] × 0 + 101
AQI = 0 + 101
AQI = 101
```

**Final AQI** = **101** (Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups - Orange)

**Another example**: PM2.5 = 45 µg/m³
```
AQI = [(150 - 101) / (55.4 - 35.5)] × (45 - 35.5) + 101
AQI = [49 / 19.9] × 9.5 + 101
AQI = 2.46 × 9.5 + 101
AQI = 23.4 + 101
AQI = 124 (Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups - Orange)
```

## AQI Categories

| AQI Range | Category | Color | What it means | Actions |
|-----------|----------|-------|---------------|---------|
| 0-50 | Good | Green | Air quality is satisfactory, minimal health risk | Ideal for outdoor activities |
| 51-100 | Moderate | Yellow | Acceptable quality, but unusually sensitive people may have concerns | Most people can be active outdoors |
| 101-150 | Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups | Orange | Sensitive groups (children, elderly, asthmatics) may experience effects | Sensitive people should limit prolonged outdoor exertion |
| 151-200 | Unhealthy | Red | Everyone may begin to experience health effects | Everyone should limit prolonged outdoor exertion |
| 201-300 | Very Unhealthy | Purple | Health alert - everyone likely to be affected | Everyone should avoid prolonged outdoor exertion |
| 301-500 | Hazardous | Maroon | Emergency conditions - serious health effects for entire population | Everyone should avoid all outdoor physical activity |

## Key Takeaways

1. **AQI simplifies complex data**: Converts multiple pollutants into one easy number
2. **6 main pollutants tracked**: PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, SO₂, CO, O₃
3. **PM2.5 is the deadliest**: Causes millions of premature deaths annually
4. **Color-coded for simplicity**: Green to Maroon indicates increasing danger
5. **Highest pollutant wins**: Report whichever pollutant has the highest AQI
6. **Fire scenario relevance**: Fires produce massive PM2.5, CO, and potentially NO₂ - critical for our project use case
7. **Real-time matters**: AQI can change hour-by-hour, especially for O₃ and NO₂
